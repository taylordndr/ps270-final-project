Introduction

Research Question

How has county type, in conjunction with gender and racial composition, impacted prison incarceration rates across American counties from 2006 to 2016?

Hypothesis

I hypothesize that the more urban an American county, the higher the prison incarceration rate for Americans living in that county will be; as urban nature increases, prison incarceration rates will increase. And I expect that this trend of higher prison incarceration rate for more urban counties will exist across all genders and races. However, I predict a disproportionately higher rate of prison incarceration for men over women, as well as for Black, Latinx and Native American individuals over White and Asian Americans as the urban nature of American counties increases. Over a 10-year time frame From 2006 to 2016, I predict incarceration rates for all the aforementioned groups to have decreased over time.

Importance

Over the course of American criminal history, Criminal Justice policies have changed drastically and prison incarceration, particularly in the late 20th and early 21st century, saw immense acceleration. However, the most current prison systems are supposedly seeing a decline in incarceration. The following study will work to analyze the “who”, “when”, and “where”, as well as a potential “why” of these modern (2006 to 2016) American incarceration trends.

Analyzing the extent to which living in an American urban center, across gender and racial lines, enhances the prison incarceration rates in that particular place will face these questions. Much research in the fields of Criminal Justice and Sociology found in the late 1900s and early 2000s patterns of increased incarceration for American cities, American ethnic centers, as opposed to in more suburban or rural areas of the country. And though more modern 21st century prisons are seeing changes in incarceration rates, disproportionate incarceration for particular places and particular groups of people has remained consistent. This is likely caused by how more economically, ethnically, and opportunity diverse a place, the increased likelihood there is for both unrest and conflict, therefore opening the door for potential adjudication.

Research on “who” is being incarcerated and “where”, as well as “why” they are being incarcerated is an important first step in understanding how American society can work to both continue lessening rates of incarceration and improving the effectiveness of punishment. Incarcerated Americans lose access to civil responsibilities, become estranged from their families and society more broadly, endure mental and physical illness, and often end up back in prison after release. Though incarceration attempts to hinder crime, Americans, especially those of male and minority communities, are being incarcerated consistently and disproportionately. Therefore, some part of the system is broken. However, if researchers can come to understand “who” is being incarcerated and “where” they are coming from, we can begin to comprehend the “why” of prison incarceration, which begins the most pivotal work on “how” we are going to further fix it moving into the future.

The Data

Description of the Data Source

This study will use the Incarceration Trends Dataset (2020) from the Vera Institute of Justice as my data source. This source draws together a multitude of studies that analyze incarceration trends in the American prison system between 1983 and 2016. The aforementioned studies are sourced from: the National Corrections Reporting Program (NCRP), the Deaths in Custody Reporting Program (DCRP), the Annual Survey of Jails (ASJ), the Census of Jails (COJ), the Census of State and Federal Adult Correctional Facilities (CAF), the FBI Uniform Crime Reporting Program (UCR), the Centers on Disease Control (CDC) (the National Vital Statistics System Data), the U.S. Department of Agriculture, and the U.S. Census Bureau. The studies contain information by American county on several variables, including: county populations, county types, prison populations, as well as general census and prison census data on both gender and racial identity.

Measuring the Independent & Dependent Variables

As mentioned, the independent and dependent variables in the following study are sourced from census prison records and general population census data collection. The main independent variable of interest in this study, is county type, referred to as “urbanicity” in the coding. County type is rated on a scale of Rural, Small/Midsize, Suburban, or Urban. The co-independent variables of interest will be Gender (Male or Female) and Race (Asian, Black, Latinx, Native American, or White).

Note: No or minimal data was available for Non-Binary or Other Race categories within the Incarceration Trends Dataset; so these groups were not included in this study.

The dependent variable is the incarceration rate, which is calculated as the proportion of incarcerated individuals from an American county (based on their Gender of Race group) per un-incarcerated individuals (between ages 15 to 64) living in that same county (the proportion taken from within the same Gender or Race group). (The age group of 15 to 64 was chosen to depict the proportion of incarceration across both the most common and most eligible prison-age population.)

It is also important to note that all representations in the following study will either be presented as being data from either 2000 or 2016, a 10-year time frame of modern American prisons, presented for comparison.

Suburban counties consistently saw the lowest rates of incarceration for men. Female incarceration did not follow the same pattern; female incarceration, generally, declined as the county type became more urban. Interpretations of Multiple Regression Models

In the first multiple regression model, holding the other county types constant at a fixed value, the coefficient on being in a Rural county (intercept (Baseline)) is 0.797, -0.128 for living in Small/Midsize counties, -0.366 for Suburban counties, and -0.119 Urban counties. Only the Rural and Suburban counties are statistically significant at the \(\alpha = 0.05\) test level. Therefore, there is a statistically significant relationship that being a Rural county, holding the other county types constant at a fixed value, increases the incarceration rate by 0.797 percentage points. There also is a statistically significant relationship that being a Suburban county, holding the other county types constant at a fixed value, decreases the incarceration rate by -0.366 percentage points.

In the second multiple regression model, holding all other county types, years, gender incarceration rates and race incarceration rates constant, Rural county type and Suburban county type have statistical significance and have the coefficients of 0.011 and -0.008 incarceration rates.

Conclusion

Overall, I did not find support for my original hypothesis that the more urban a county, the higher the incarceration rate. Based on the graphs, my main findings were as follows: over time and county type, Male incarceration is consistently significantly higher than Female incarceration, Men saw higher incarceration rates in Rural and Urban counties, female incarceration typically declined as the county type became more urban, the AAPI community always had the lowest incarceration versus other racial groupings, Black Americans saw the highest incarceration rates across all Small/Midsize, Suburban and Urban county types versus other racial groupings, Native American incarceration rate increased significantly into 2016, and across races Rural or Small/Midsize counties trended to have higher incarceration rates in each racial group than Urban or Suburban counties.

Based on the regression model of my main coefficient, there are statistically significant relationships (at the \(\alpha = 0.05\) test level) for Rural and Suburban county types. Rural counties had an increase in incarceration rate by 0.797 percentage points, holding the other county types constant. Suburban county type, decreases the incarceration rate by -0.366 percentage points, holding the other county types constant. These trends were also found when holding all the confounders I accounted for in the graphs constant. Based on the confirmation of statistical significance (rejection of the null) in both these findings, in addition to the interpretations of the graphs in this study, I would say these two relationships have causal effect. Living in a Rural county has a positive impact on incarceration rates and being from a Suburban county has a negative impact on incarceration rates.

There are two main limitations in this study; one based off missing data confounders and another on other potential confounding variables. As for missing data, there was minimal or no data on other gender identities, other racial groups and the data in some county types, particularly Rural counties, was limited. Having too small a data pool can conflate and undermine certain data predictions, as can be seen with the inconsistency of racial incarceration rates between 2006 and 2016. As for other confounding variables, it is likely financial status, political leaning and education level could be impacting incarceration rates, as these factors typically lead to different lifestyles in both Rural and Suburban communities.

With more time and money, I could improve these analyses mainly through collecting more data. Collecting data on the aforementioned confounding variables: financial status, political leaning and education would lead more conclusively to what about Rural communities contributes to higher incarceration and what about Suburban communities leads to lower incarceration. Further analyses could also consider these factors across Black and Native American communities to understand their disproportionate levels of prison incarceration. Conducting these additional analyses could aid in forming policy: monetary and welfare policy, education programming and funding. Policies which could work to solve the incarceration problem in the United States from the roots.
